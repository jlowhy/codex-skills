# Interview Guide

Use this guide to run the grill as a staged conversation. Ask one question at a time, keep the whole stage in mind, and treat critique plus recommendation as working methods. For every unresolved decision, recommend an answer, explain why it serves the reader, goal, evidence, length, and style target, and name the tradeoff.

## 1. Intake

Establish what exists: no idea, rough idea, notes, draft, outline, screenshots, transcript, docs, codebase, articles, or prior writing. Identify what is missing enough to block progress: reader, desired effect, references, length, or available proof.

If source material is available, inspect it first and report the useful facts, likely claims, and open gaps.

If starting from an existing piece without prior context, reconstruct the apparent format, venue, reader, reader problem or resistance, thesis, desired reader change, assumptions, and missing context. Present these as inferences, not facts, then ask the one question whose answer would most change the argument map or sharpening direction.

## 2. Orientation

Establish what the writing is for:

- Format: article, essay, social post, thread, launch post, memo, speech, email, or other.
- Occasion: why this needs to exist now.
- Reader: who this is for, what they already believe, and what they are likely missing.
- Desired effect: what the reader should think, feel, decide, say, or do afterward.
- Length: how long the piece can be, how long it should be, and what the venue rewards.
- References: sources for facts and claims, plus style examples for voice, venue norms, pacing, density, and what to avoid.
- Constraints: audience sensitivity, deadline, approval needs, or source boundaries.

Core questions:

1. **Essence**: What is the one sentence the piece exists to make true?
2. **Reader**: Who needs this, and what are they likely wrong about or missing?
3. **Stakes**: What happens if the reader keeps believing the old version?
4. **Tension**: What pressure, contradiction, or unresolved question creates momentum?
5. **Evidence**: What concrete story, example, data, scene, or observation earns the claim?
6. **Turn**: Where does the piece become more interesting than the reader expected?

## 3. Reference Calibration

Use references for evidence, argument grounding, and style calibration: facts, examples, data, code behavior, prior claims, voice, density, pacing, structure, sentence energy, and what not to imitate.

Validate which claims are sourced, inferred, or speculative; which sections need more research; which style traits are useful instead of surface mimicry; and whether the length fits the evidence and expected density.

## 4. Point-Form Argument Map

Turn the orientation into a complete point-form map before drafting prose:

1. Name the narrative shape that best fits the material.
2. Propose the section or beat sequence.
3. For each section, map: reader cares about, point to make, why it matters, evidence needed, and risk to avoid.
4. Check whether the sequence tracks the reader's mental state, not just the writer's content plan.
5. Mark unsupported claims, missing research, style mismatches, and length pressure.
6. Identify the weakest dependency, missing proof, or sequence problem.
If the sequence is a flat list, force progression. Each section should change what the reader can understand, not merely add another point.

Stay in point form until the whole map is coherent and the user agrees to write prose. If the user starts drafting too early, pause and return to the map.

## 5. Pressure Test

Before fleshing out the piece, attack the current point-form map:

- **Thesis**: too obvious, vague, broad, or not worth the reader's time.
- **Reader**: likely to not care, resist, misunderstand, or already know it.
- **Evidence**: weakest proof or claim a skeptical reader would reject.
- **Structure**: drag, repetition, over-explanation, or missing bridge.
- **Style/length**: voice, venue fit, borrowed texture, overreach, or underuse of space.

For each objection, decide whether to fix the plan, gather more evidence, narrow the claim, change the reader, change the length, or cut the weak part. Do not proceed by merely noting the risk.

- If the idea is generic, force the lived observation, scene, example, or conflict that makes it specific.
- If it is just advice, force the story or contradiction behind it; if it is only a story, force the claim it earns.
- If friction appears everywhere, consolidate it into one structural tension.
- If the audience is broad, narrow it to the reader whose mind can realistically change.
- If the ending repeats the opening, push for a turn, implication, or unresolved resonance.
- If the claim survives only because it is vague, force a sharper claim that can be attacked.

## 6. Flesh

After the argument map makes sense, develop content one section at a time:

1. Confirm the section's job, core claim, and role in the spine.
2. Choose the evidence, scene, example, claim, or beat.
3. Draft or outline only that section.
4. If actual prose exists, use `WRITING-REVIEW.md` to inspect the writing itself before continuing.
5. Tighten sequence and specificity at the section level before expression cleanup.

Do not write the entire piece in one pass unless the user explicitly asks. If prose exposes a structural or evidence problem, return to the argument map or reference calibration.

## 7. Sharpening Grill

After structure and substance are clear, sharpen the prose by grilling it:

1. Identify the passage's job, reader, intended effect, core claim, and constraints to preserve.
2. Attack weak expression: filler, vague synthesis, throat-clearing, over-polish, generic elevation, awkward sequence, and lost cadence.
3. Apply the smallest rewrite that improves force, clarity, specificity, sequence, or sentence economy without changing meaning.
4. Explain what changed, what was cut, and the tradeoff.
5. Preserve useful imperfection, strong specificity, personal judgment, and technical precision.
For substantial rewrites, produce distinct versions by intent, not tiny wording variants, then recommend one and explain why.
